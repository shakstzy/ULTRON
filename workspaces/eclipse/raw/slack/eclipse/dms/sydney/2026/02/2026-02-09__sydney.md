# DM with sydney — 2026-02-09 (Monday)

## 15:17 — Adithya Kumar (me)

How do you typically structure your data licensing deals so that data owners feel comfortable with the risk and “sketchiness” around licensing? Any specific terms or protections that make a big difference?

For Troveo (or similar companies), what messages, proof points, or brand signals have been most important in convincing partners to say yes? For example, product maturity, logos, security/compliance, rev share, etc.

When prospects say no after an initial positive response, what are the most common objections you hear, and how do you usually handle or pre‑empt those in your outreach and contracts?

> ### 18:13 — sydney
> 
> Hey, really good questions and it's really a multi pronged strategy:
> - Our experience has been that "revenue washes all sins". So anything you can share to content owners on the deal pipeline and visibility into payouts will end up being the key decision making criteria.
> - Some licensors actually won't budge unless you provide minimum guarantees (MGs) though this is very effective when used strategically
> - The smaller guys are probably looking for signals that others in their space are doing the same thing. I know early on Troveo had a really strong ground game on Reddit amongst content creators
> - Along those same lines of the social signals, bringing on someone who knows the space and is credible / charismatic / can speak to that audience is critical. Our Head of Content David has unlocked so much value that don't think we wouldve gotten here without him
> - We also have had success on both supply & demand side referral agreements. Going to industry partners and giving them a % cut of deals we sign
> - In general though we've found it to be tougher to work with smaller individual content owners which is why we've pivoted to much larger media companies/studios/brands
> - I can see this being especially challenging with podcasters since it's their actual voice that would be used in training. Probably a lot more discomfort with this
> - One potential way to disarm this is stating that the content is ultimately anonymized and not being trained on their (voice) likeness. Most use cases are def more for audio understanding and fine tuning failure modes but what most people's minds go to is like OpenAI using Scarlett Johansen's voice or something and will their voice end up being a voice assistant
> - Not sure if that's really the case or not though so you'd have to validate that
>
>
> In summary I think the most important thing is signaling credibility in the space through the right people/partners/channels and the content owners believing that they will get paid'
>
