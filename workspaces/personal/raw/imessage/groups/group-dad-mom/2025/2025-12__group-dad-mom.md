## ✅ What could be “good” (or potentially useful)

* **Legitimate medical use.** Methylene Blue *is* an approved medication — but only for specific conditions. For example, it’s used to treat Methemoglobinemia (a rare blood disorder that affects oxygen delivery). ([National Geographic][1])
* **Possible cellular / mitochondrial effects (theoretical interest).** Some research suggests that MB has redox‑cycling properties — meaning it can accept and donate electrons — which might help optimize mitochondrial function, reduce oxidative stress, and in theory support cellular energy production. ([Drugs.com][2])
* **Research interest, not proof.** Because of those biochemical properties, scientists have studied MB for things like cognitive support, anti‑aging, or neuroprotection. But — *so far* — evidence in humans is very limited. ([Drugs.com][2])

👉 In short, MB has real medical value — but that doesn’t automatically extend to “wellness supplement” use.

## ⚠️ What’s “bad” or risky

* **Serious side effects & drug interactions.** MB is a monoamine‑oxidase inhibitor (MAOI). If someone takes it along with common antidepressants or other serotonergic drugs (SSRIs, SNRIs, etc.), that can trigger Serotonin Syndrome — a potentially life‑threatening condition. ([WebMD][3])
* **Not safe for everyone.** People with a genetic condition called G6PD deficiency may suffer dangerous hemolytic anemia (red‑blood‑cell breakdown) if they take MB. ([WebMD][3])
* **Common side effects even in “safer” contexts.** These include blue‑green discoloration of urine/body fluids, nausea, dizziness, headache, changes in taste, possibly increased sensitivity to light (photosensitivity), among others. ([Drugs.com][4])
* **Quality / purity concerns if sourced outside medical channels.** MB originally was a dye and lab chemical; many products marketed as “supplements” may not be pharmaceutical‑grade. Some “dye‑grade” or “aquarium‑grade” MB are unsafe for human ingestion. ([Harvard Health][5])

## 🚫 What’s “ugly” — when things go wrong

* **Potentially life‑threatening reactions.** In cases of interactions (especially with antidepressants) or in vulnerable individuals (G6PD deficiency, pregnancy, etc.), MB can cause serious harm — hemolysis, serotonin syndrome, cardiovascular strain, and other severe reactions. ([NCBI][6])
* **Lack of evidence for “wellness” uses.** Despite hype around MB for nootropics, anti-aging, or energy, human clinical data is lacking. Most supportive data comes from cell or animal studies; experts overwhelmingly advise caution. ([National Geographic][1])
* **You often don’t know what you’re getting.** Online MB supplements may vary widely in purity, dosage, and safety. Because they’re not regulated like prescription drugs, there’s no guarantee what’s in the capsule. ([Harvard Health][5])
* **Risk of harmful effects getting worse than the original problem.** Paradoxically, at high or inappropriate doses, MB can itself cause or worsen conditions like methemoglobinemia (the very disorder it's approved to treat). ([National Geographic][1])

## 🎯 The Bottom Line — "Good / Bad / Ugly" Verdict

* MB has legitimate, narrow medical uses.
* The theoretical benefits (cognitive, anti‑aging, mitochondrial) remain speculative for most humans — without robust clinical proof.
* Off‑label use (capsules sold as supplements) carries real risk — especially for people on medications, with underlying conditions, or uncertain about what they’re ingesting.
* Unless you have a clear, medically supervised indication (like methemoglobinemia), using MB “just for wellness” is risky, poorly studied, and potentially dangerous.
